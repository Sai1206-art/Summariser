# Bored to Sleep: The Dullest History of Trains Ever Told

*A sleep audiobook in twenty chapters.*

**Production notes (do not narrate):** Narrate at reduced speed (about minus twenty percent), flat and warm, with long pauses at paragraph breaks. No music necessary; optional soft rain bed under narration. Each chapter is a self-contained sleep session of five to seven minutes and ends with "Goodnight." Never raise energy, even for exciting events. The facts are real; the delivery is deliberately unhurried.

---

## Opening: How to Listen to This Book

Welcome to Bored to Sleep. This is a history book that does not mind if you miss it.

You do not need to remember anything. There will be no test. If you fall asleep during chapter two, the trains will still be there tomorrow, moving slowly, as trains do.

Get comfortable. Let the day finish itself somewhere else. Wherever you stopped listening last night is exactly the right place to begin.

Tonight, and every night, we will talk about trains. Slowly. In more detail than anyone has ever asked for.

## Chapter One: The Unremarkable Origins of the Wooden Rail

There is nothing you need to do right now. There is nothing you need to remember. Our story begins, as so many dull stories do, with a piece of wood.

In the mining valleys of sixteenth-century Germany, workers laid simple wooden planks along the ground, so that heavy carts full of ore could roll more easily. The planks were flat. The carts were slow. The ore was heavy. And that, for about two hundred years, was more or less the entire story.

The wooden rails wore down, of course. Wood does that. So the miners replaced them. And then those wore down too. And so they replaced them again. Year after year. Plank after plank. It was not exciting work, and nobody wrote poems about it.

In England, in the collieries of Northumberland, the wooden wagonways grew a little longer. A cart would roll gently downhill from the mine to the river, guided by the rails, while a patient horse walked behind. At the river, the coal was unloaded. Then the horse pulled the empty cart back up the hill. Down, and back up. Down, and back up. The horse did not mind. The horse had nowhere else to be. And neither, right now, do you.

Around the year seventeen sixty-seven, someone had the quiet idea of covering the wooden rails with thin strips of iron, so they would last a little longer. The strips were called plates, and the men who laid them were called platelayers, a job title that has survived to this day, long after anyone stopped finding it interesting.

The iron plates were better than wood. Not dramatically better. Just better enough that people kept using them. Progress, in the history of railways, was rarely a lightning bolt. It was more like a long, slow afternoon in which somebody, somewhere, made a cart roll very slightly more smoothly than before.

Let your shoulders soften, the way an old wooden rail softens under two hundred years of patient wheels.

By the end of the eighteenth century, there were hundreds of miles of these little wagonways, quietly connecting mines to rivers, rivers to ports. No timetables. No stations. No tickets. Just carts, rolling gently along, at the speed of a walking horse, carrying coal that would warm houses whose owners never once thought about the rails.

And somewhere in Cornwall, a young engineer named Richard Trevithick was beginning to wonder whether steam might push a cart along those rails all by itself. But that is a story for the next chapter. There is no hurry to get there. The rails have waited two hundred years. They can wait one more night.

The planks are laid. The carts are rolling. The horse is walking slowly home.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Two: Richard Trevithick and the Engine Nobody Kept

If you are still awake, that is perfectly fine. If you are half asleep, that is even better. Tonight we meet Richard Trevithick, a very large man from Cornwall who built a very heavy engine that worked almost well enough.

Trevithick grew up among the tin mines, where steam engines pumped water slowly out of the ground, day and night, nodding up and down like enormous iron beasts that had learned exactly one trick. He understood steam the way other people understand weather. He simply lived inside it.

In the year eighteen oh four, at an ironworks called Penydarren, in Wales, Trevithick did something that had never been done before. He set a steam engine on rails, attached some wagons full of iron and some curious passengers, and let it pull them along. Nine miles and three quarters. It took a little over four hours. You could have walked it faster, if you were in a hurry. Nobody on the train was in a hurry.

It was, by every reasonable definition, the first railway journey by steam locomotive in the history of the world. And here is the wonderfully dull part: hardly anyone cared.

The engine was so heavy that it cracked the cast-iron rails beneath it. Plate after plate, quietly breaking under all that ambition. So the ironworks took the engine off its wheels and used it to power a hammer instead. The first locomotive in history spent most of its life standing still, doing something else.

Trevithick tried again. In eighteen oh eight, he set up a small circular track in London and ran an engine around it, around and around, charging a shilling a ride. He called the engine Catch Me Who Can. Very few people tried to catch it. The rails broke again. The public shrugged. Trevithick packed up his circle and moved on.

Notice how your breathing has settled into its own gentle rhythm. In, and out. Around, and around. Like a small engine on a circular track, going nowhere in particular, in no particular hurry.

Trevithick eventually sailed off to Peru to work on mining engines, had a long series of adventures that we will not describe because they were interesting, and came home with almost nothing. He died without much money, and other men became famous for the railway.

But every train that has ever run owes something to that slow morning in Wales, when a heavy engine crawled nine miles with its load of iron, cracking the rails politely behind it, at walking pace, while the world declined to notice.

The engine has stopped. The steam is settling. The world is declining to notice you, too, in the kindest possible way.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Three: Puffing Billy and the Slow Years

Between the first locomotive and the first famous locomotive, there were about twenty years in which not very much happened. This chapter is about those years. It is one of the dullest chapters in the book, and I say that with real affection.

The problem, you may remember, was that engines were heavy and rails were brittle. Engineers responded to this problem slowly, in small steps, which is how engineers respond to most things.

In Leeds, a man named John Blenkinsop decided the answer was a toothed wheel. His engines climbed along a rack of iron teeth beside the rail, click by click, hauling coal from the Middleton pit into the city. From eighteen twelve onward they did this every working day, clicking and puffing, puffing and clicking, entirely without glamour. They were the first locomotives anywhere to earn their keep, and they did it the way most of us earn ours: repetitively.

At Wylam colliery, near Newcastle, the engineer William Hedley took a different view. He believed smooth wheels on smooth rails would grip perfectly well, if the weight was spread out properly. In eighteen thirteen his engine appeared, and the villagers named it Puffing Billy, because it puffed, and because naming things was simpler then.

Puffing Billy worked at that colliery, hauling coal wagons at about five miles an hour, for nearly fifty years. Think about that, as your eyes grow heavy. Fifty years. The same short stretch of track. The same coal. The same gentle puffing, morning after morning, through every season, while empires rose and fell somewhere over the horizon. Puffing Billy did not follow the news.

Today, Puffing Billy stands in the Science Museum in London, the oldest surviving steam locomotive in the world. It does not move anymore. It never moved much to begin with.

And in the village of Wylam itself, in a small stone cottage beside the wagonway, a boy had grown up watching the horse-drawn coal carts roll past his front door. His name was George Stephenson. He did not learn to read until he was eighteen. He paid for his own night-school lessons out of his colliery wages, fixing shoes and mending clocks on the side.

By day he tended the winding engines at the pit. By night, he studied. Slowly, patiently, the way iron rails are laid: one length at a time.

We will follow him tomorrow night. For now, the rack rail is clicking softly, the smooth wheels are turning, and Puffing Billy is puffing somewhere in the distance, at five miles an hour, with fifty unhurried years ahead of it.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Four: George Stephenson and the Stockton and Darlington Railway

Tonight, the story becomes slightly famous. Do not worry. We will keep it slow.

George Stephenson, the boy from the cottage beside the wagonway, had become an engine-wright — a fixer of pit machinery, a practical man with coal dust in every line of his hands. In eighteen fourteen he built his first locomotive, named Blücher, which hauled coal uphill at four miles an hour and was, by the standards of the day, adequate.

Adequate was enough. Stephenson kept building. Engine after engine, each one slightly less likely to break than the last. He also designed a miners' safety lamp, and argued about it with a famous chemist, but that argument was interesting, so we will leave it out.

Meanwhile, in the town of Darlington, a Quaker wool merchant named Edward Pease wanted a better way to move coal to the port at Stockton. The sensible options were a canal, or a horse-drawn tramway. Pease met Stephenson. Stephenson, in his heavy Northumbrian accent, suggested steam. Pease, to his eternal credit, listened patiently.

The Stockton and Darlington Railway opened on the twenty-seventh of September, eighteen twenty-five. Stephenson himself drove the engine, called Locomotion Number One, pulling wagons of coal and flour, a coach for dignitaries, and hundreds of people who had climbed aboard uninvited and refused to get off. A man on a horse rode ahead carrying a flag, because nobody was entirely sure the engine would stop.

The train reached fifteen miles an hour on the good stretches. Thousands of people lined the route to watch coal wagons move slightly faster than a horse. It was, at the time, the most exciting thing that had ever happened in Darlington. It would not excite you now, and that is exactly why it belongs in this book.

Here is the truly soothing part. For years afterward, the railway was not what you would imagine. Steam engines pulled the coal, but passengers rode in horse-drawn coaches that ran along the same rails, fitted with railway wheels. Innkeepers operated them, the way they had operated stagecoaches. If two coaches met head-on on the single track, the drivers argued about who should reverse to the nearest passing loop. Sometimes they argued for quite a while. The railway timetable, in its infancy, included human stubbornness as a scheduled feature.

Slowly, the horses were retired. Slowly, the arguments faded. Slowly, the railway became a railway.

Feel yourself settling, the way a new railway settles: gradually, with occasional gentle disputes, into something calm and regular and quietly useful.

The flag has been furled. The horse has gone home. Locomotion Number One is cooling in its shed.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Five: The Rainhill Trials, Described as Calmly as Possible

In October of eighteen twenty-nine, on a level stretch of track at Rainhill, near Liverpool, there occurred one of the most dramatic events in railway history. I am going to describe it in the least dramatic way I can. This is, after all, a bedtime story.

The new Liverpool and Manchester Railway was nearly finished, and its directors could not agree on what should pull the trains. Some wanted stationary engines hauling carriages on long ropes. Some wanted locomotives. So they announced a competition, with a prize of five hundred pounds, to find out whether any locomotive was up to the job.

The rules were long and fussy, and I will share only a little of their fussiness with you, as a treat. Each engine had to weigh no more than six tons. It had to consume its own smoke. It had to pull three times its weight, back and forth along the course, for a distance equal to a return trip between the two cities. Judges with notebooks would time everything. There were, reportedly, ten thousand spectators, most of whom could not see very much.

Five machines entered. One, called the Cycloped, was powered by a horse walking on a belt, and we may quietly set it aside. Timothy Hackworth's Sans Pareil burned coal enthusiastically and broke down. The Novelty, a light and elegant London machine, was the crowd's darling; it went beautifully fast, twice, and then stopped going at all.

And then there was the Rocket, built by George Stephenson's son, Robert — a quieter, better-educated man than his father, and possibly the finest engineer of the two. The Rocket contained one genuinely important idea: instead of one big pipe carrying hot gas through the boiler, it had twenty-five small ones, which turned water to steam far more efficiently. This idea is called the multi-tubular boiler. You do not need to remember it. It has been remembered by others.

The Rocket did not break down. That was its entire strategy, and it is a strategy I recommend to everyone. It trundled back and forth, back and forth, averaging around fourteen miles an hour, occasionally showing off at nearly thirty, while its rivals sat in pieces beside the track. Back, and forth. Back, and forth. The judges filled their notebooks. The crowd drifted home.

The Rocket won the five hundred pounds, and every locomotive built since is, in some quiet sense, its grandchild.

Let the day's competitions leave you now. You do not need to be the fastest. You only need to keep gently going, back and forth, until the judges fall asleep.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Six: The Liverpool and Manchester, and the Beginning of Ordinary

The Liverpool and Manchester Railway opened on the fifteenth of September, eighteen thirty. The opening day itself was eventful, and in one case tragic, and because this is a bedtime story we will let the historians keep that day, and move directly to the part they usually skip: every other day.

Because the truly remarkable thing about the Liverpool and Manchester was not its opening. It was its Tuesday mornings.

It was the first railway in the world to connect two major cities. The first to run entirely on steam. The first to carry mail. The first to run to a timetable, with trains leaving whether or not anyone important was aboard. For the first time in human history, an ordinary person could stand in one city in the morning and be in another by lunch, without a horse being involved in any way.

And almost immediately, people got used to it.

That is the part I find most soothing. Within a few years, the miracle had become furniture. Commuters complained about delays on a service that would have seemed like sorcery to their grandparents. The trains ran, and ran, and ran. Thirty miles of track, back and forth between Liverpool and Manchester, carrying cotton and coal and clerks, in rain, in fog, in the pale northern sunshine.

The railway had two classes of carriage at first. First class had roofs. Second class, for a while, did not, which made the English weather a member of the crew. Eventually everyone got a roof. Progress, once again, arriving quietly and slightly late.

The engines needed water every few miles, and coke, and constant small repairs. Men stood along the line all day, watching the rails, tapping things with hammers. A whole new profession appeared: people whose job was simply to make sure that tomorrow was exactly like today.

Let that be tonight's lullaby. Somewhere, for nearly two hundred years now, someone has been quietly making sure that tomorrow's trains run exactly like today's. They are probably doing it right now, in the dark, with a flask of tea. You are allowed to sleep. The rails are being watched.

The cotton is loaded. The clerks have gone home. The timetable is pinned to the wall, promising an ordinary morning.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Seven: The Gauge Wars, a Very Polite Disaster

Tonight's subject is the width between two rails. If that sentence alone has made your eyelids heavier, my work is nearly done. But stay with me a little, because this particular width caused twenty years of trouble, all of it slow.

George Stephenson built his railways with the rails four feet, eight and a half inches apart, because that was roughly the width of the colliery wagonways he grew up with. There was no grand theory behind it. It was simply the size things already were, which is the reason behind more of the world than we like to admit.

But Isambard Kingdom Brunel, the brilliant engineer of the Great Western Railway, looked at those four feet, eight and a half inches, and found them small. Brunel did not like small things. He built his railway from London to Bristol with the rails seven feet and a quarter of an inch apart. The trains were wider, steadier, faster. Magnificent, even. There was only one problem, and it approached at the speed of laid track: eventually, the two systems would meet.

They met at Gloucester, in eighteen forty-four. And nothing dramatic happened. That is the beauty of it. The trains simply stopped, on their differently spaced rails, and could go no further. Every passenger, every trunk, every crate of goods, every anxious hen in its basket had to be carried across the platform from one train to the other. Porters shuttled back and forth, back and forth. Luggage was mislaid at scale. The hens expressed their views.

A Royal Commission was appointed, because this was Britain. Learned men held races between broad and narrow gauge engines and wrote a long report. The broad gauge was, they admitted, rather better. But there was five times as much narrow track already laid, and so, in the great tradition of committees, the answer was: keep the one there is more of. The Gauge Act of eighteen forty-six made Stephenson's measurement the standard.

The broad gauge did not vanish overnight. It faded politely, over almost fifty years, until one weekend in May, eighteen ninety-two, when thousands of workmen knelt along the Great Western main line and moved one rail closer to the other, mile after mile after mile, and the argument was over.

Today, more than half the railways on Earth still use four feet, eight and a half inches — the width of a coal cart from a village in Northumberland, carried around the world by habit.

Let your own measurements rest now. Whatever width today insisted on, it can be re-laid tomorrow.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Eight: The Navvies, Who Dug

Every railway in this book was, before it was anything else, a very long hole. Tonight we honor the men who dug it, in the most restful way possible: by describing shovelling at length.

They were called navvies, short for navigators, a name inherited from the men who had dug the canals a generation earlier. At the height of the railway age, a quarter of a million of them were at work in Britain alone — more men than the army — digging their way across the countryside with picks, shovels, and wheelbarrows.

A railway wants to be level. Hills object. So the navvies removed the hills, one barrowload at a time, and poured them into the valleys. A cutting here, an embankment there, on and on across the shires. It has been estimated that a good navvy shifted around twenty tons of earth in a day. Twenty tons. By hand. And then came back the next morning and did it again.

Listen to the rhythm of that work, because it is the rhythm of this whole book. Dig, lift, wheel, tip. Dig, lift, wheel, tip. A hundred times before lunch. A thousand strong men moving a hillside a shovelful at a time, slowly enough that from a distance nothing seemed to be happening at all. And yet, in thirty years, they laid enough railway across Britain to reach most of the way around the Earth.

They lived in turf huts beside the works, moving on when the line moved on. They had magnificent nicknames — every history mentions them — and they consumed beef and beer in quantities that alarmed local magistrates. Respectable Victorians wrote pamphlets worrying about them. The navvies did not read the pamphlets. They were asleep by dark, being tired, from the twenty tons.

Their greatest works you can still stand inside. Box Tunnel, near Bath, nearly two miles through solid stone, dug from both ends by candlelight and meeting in the middle almost exactly where the drawings promised. The great cuttings at Tring and Roade, sixty feet deep, walked out of the ground barrow by barrow.

Modern engineers, with satellites and lasers, still follow the paths the navvies made, because the earth, once moved, stays moved.

Your own day's digging is finished now. Whatever you shifted today — words, boxes, worries — set the shovel down. The embankment will hold until morning.

Dig, lift, wheel, tip. Dig, lift, wheel... tip.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Nine: Railway Time, or How the Clocks Agreed

Here is a fact so quietly enormous that it usually escapes notice: before the railways, the time was different in every town, and nobody minded.

Noon, after all, is when the sun is highest. The sun reaches its height over Bristol about ten minutes after it does over London, and so Bristol noon was ten minutes after London noon, and Exeter was a few minutes behind that. Every town kept the sun's own local time, measured by its own church clock, and since the fastest thing between towns was a horse, the differences never collided. If your coach took eighteen hours, nobody asked which ten minutes it was when you arrived.

Then the trains came, and the minutes began to matter.

A timetable is a promise about time, and a promise is difficult to keep in a country with hundreds of different noons. Worse, on a single-track railway, two trains might be sharing one line, kept apart by nothing but the schedule. A ten-minute disagreement between clocks was no longer a curiosity.

So, beginning in eighteen forty, the railway companies did something both sensible and strange: they decided what time it was. The Great Western led the way, ordering that every station clock along its line should keep London time — Greenwich time — regardless of what the sun above that station had to say about it.

Slowly, town by town, the sun lost the argument. The time signal traveled along the new electric telegraph wires that ran beside the tracks, so that every station master could set his clock by Greenwich each morning. People called it railway time. For years, some town clocks wore two minute hands, one for local sun time and one for the railway — two opinions about the present moment, ticking politely side by side.

Dean of Exeter and assorted traditionalists grumbled. The sun, they felt, outranked the timetable. But the trains kept arriving on railway time, and it is hard to argue with something that keeps arriving. In eighteen eighty, Parliament made Greenwich time the legal time for all of Britain, and the second minute hands came quietly down.

So tonight, when you glance at a clock — though I suggest you don't — remember that the very idea of a shared "now" was built for the convenience of trains.

The trains have all arrived. The telegraph is silent. And for you, alone in the dark, there is no schedule at all. The sun will manage noon without you.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Ten: Bradshaw's Guide, the Most Boring Bestseller Ever Printed

Tonight I want to tell you about a book. Not this book, though the family resemblance is strong. A book of timetables.

George Bradshaw was a Quaker mapmaker from Manchester, a precise and modest man. In eighteen thirty-nine, he began printing the railway timetables of Britain, gathered together in one small volume. In eighteen forty-one this became Bradshaw's Monthly Railway Guide: hundreds of pages of departure times, arrival times, junctions, fares, and footnotes, in type small enough to make a watchmaker squint.

It was, by every account, bewildering. Punch magazine made jokes about it for decades — mostly the same joke, which is that no living person could understand it. Column after column of figures. Asterisks that referred to daggers. Daggers that referred to notes. Notes that referred, with quiet menace, to other pages. Trains that ran on Tuesdays only, or not in December, or only as far as the junction, where a connection might, or might not, be waiting.

And yet everyone owned one. For over a century — the Guide was published, monthly, from eighteen forty-one until nineteen sixty-one — Bradshaw was simply how Britain moved. Sherlock Holmes consults it in the stories. Phileas Fogg travels with one. Anxious aunts across the empire sat by lamplight, tracing a Tuesday-only train with one finger, planning journeys they would worry about for weeks.

Think of the sheer patience compressed into those pages. Some clerk, somewhere, verified every one of those thousands of figures, every single month, by hand, for one hundred and twenty years. The columns were checked, corrected, reset in tiny type, and posted to every stationer in the land. Then the next month began, and they did it all again. If that clerk could keep doing that, you can certainly manage falling asleep, which requires checking nothing.

Here is your bedtime timetable, in the style of Bradshaw. The nine-forty-two eyelid service is now closing its doors. Passengers for deeper sleep should remain where they are; the connection arrives on its own. The Tuesday-only train of worry has been cancelled, without replacement. See footnote. The footnote says: rest.

All services are running to time. No changes are expected before morning.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Eleven: Signals, and the Men Who Waved Them

Tonight's chapter is about how trains avoid meeting each other, which they accomplish by means of flags, arms, bells, and an enormous amount of standing still. It is the perfect subject for us, because the entire science of railway signalling comes down to one instruction, repeated forever: wait.

In the very early years, the method was a man. He was called a railway policeman, and he stood beside the track with flags, or simply with his arms held in agreed positions. After a train passed, he counted the minutes, and until enough had passed, he told the next train: wait. His only tools were a watch and his own patience, and railwaymen call signals "bobbies" to this day in his memory.

The trouble with time, as a safety system, is that it assumes the first train is still moving. If it had quietly broken down around the bend, the minutes ticked on regardless. Something better was needed, and the telegraph provided it: the block system. The line was divided into sections, and the iron rule was one train in one block at a time. A signalman telegraphed the box ahead: train entering your block. And until the box behind rang back — train has left, all clear — nothing else got in. Bell codes rang up and down the line all day: one beat, pause, two beats. Train entering. Train clear. Wait. Proceed. Wait.

To show the driver all this, the semaphore signal was invented: a wooden arm on a tall post. Arm raised or lowered at an angle: proceed. Arm straight out: stop. At night, colored lamps did the work — and it took some sorrow to settle on the colors. Red for stop, that was always plain. But for many years white meant clear, until it was understood that a broken red lens also shows white, and says "come ahead" precisely when it shouldn't. So green, once the color of caution, was promoted to mean all clear, and it has meant that ever since — on railways, and then on roads, and now at every traffic light on Earth. When the lights change tomorrow, they will be speaking railway.

The signalmen themselves worked alone, in high glass boxes, pulling great steel levers polished by decades of hands. Between trains, there was nothing to do, and doing nothing was the job. They kept kettles. They kept cats. They wrote every bell code in a ledger, in careful copperplate, hour after quiet hour.

Sit in the signal box with them now. The lamp is lit. The levers are set. Somewhere down the line, a bell rings once: train entering. It is not your train. Nothing is expected in your block tonight.

The arm is raised. The light is green. You may proceed, very slowly, into sleep.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Twelve: Crossing America, One Spike at a Time

Tonight we leave Britain and go to America, where everything was the same, only much, much longer.

By the eighteen sixties, the eastern United States had plenty of railways, and California, on the far side, had gold, farms, and ambitions. Between them lay nearly two thousand miles of prairie, desert, and mountain. The plan, authorized by Congress in eighteen sixty-two, was elegantly simple: one company would build west from the Missouri River, another would build east from Sacramento, and somewhere in the middle of all that emptiness, they would meet.

The Union Pacific built west across the plains, which sounds easy and was merely endless. Flat mile after flat mile of grading, tie-laying, and spiking, through summers of dust and winters that stopped everything. The Central Pacific built east, and ran almost immediately into the Sierra Nevada, a granite wall the height of a mile. Their workforce — thousands upon thousands of Chinese laborers, who did the hardest and least thanked work on the whole line — cut fifteen tunnels through the mountains with hand drills and black powder. In places the granite was so stubborn that a full day's blasting advanced the tunnel eight inches. Eight inches. A day. Whole seasons were spent inside one dark hole in a mountain, and the answer to "how far today?" was: about the length of your hand.

Let your breath slow to granite pace. There is no hurry that means anything to a mountain.

Out on the plains, the work found its rhythm. Rails forward, ties down, spikes in. Three strokes to a spike, ten spikes to a rail. The two railroads were paid by the mile, so they raced — at walking speed. Near the end, the Central Pacific's crews laid ten miles of track in a single day, a record set with hammers and shoulders, and one that no one has ever quite matched since.

They met at last at Promontory Summit, Utah, on the tenth of May, eighteen sixty-nine. A golden spike was tapped gently into a polished laurel tie, speeches were endured, and the telegraph flashed one word to both coasts: done. The nation celebrated. Then workmen quietly removed the golden spike — gold being soft, and valuable — and drove an ordinary iron one in its place. The famous spike went to a museum. The ordinary spike went to work, holding the rail, unphotographed, for years.

Be the iron spike tonight, not the golden one. Set, settled, holding quietly, with the celebrations over and nothing further asked of you.

The coasts are joined. The telegraph has said its one word. The prairie is dark and very, very wide.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Thirteen: Mr. Pullman's Beds on Wheels

Of all the chapters in this book, this one belongs here most of all. Tonight: the history of sleeping on trains.

Early rail travel and sleep were not acquainted. The first sleeping arrangements, in America in the eighteen forties, were bare wooden shelves in a swaying carriage, stacked three high, on which gentlemen lay down fully dressed, boots and all, and listened to each other regret the journey. Bedding was sometimes provided, but you shared it with whoever, or whatever, had used it before you.

George Mortimer Pullman, a cabinetmaker and engineer from New York State, rode one of those shelves one night and, according to the story, could not sleep at all. He then did what no one else had thought worth doing: he took the problem seriously.

Pullman decided a railway carriage could be a good hotel, rather than a moving shelf. His car, the Pioneer, finished in eighteen sixty-five, cost about five times what anyone had ever spent on a carriage, and it showed. Polished walnut. Proper mattresses. Clean linen — clean every time. By day the beds vanished: the lower berth folded out of the seats, and the upper berth folded up flat against the ceiling, so cleverly hinged that the room forgot it was a bedroom. At night the porter unfolded the world again: seats to bed, ceiling to bunk, curtains drawn, lamps low.

The Pullman porters deserve a chapter of their own patience. Almost all of them were Black men, many formerly enslaved, working immensely long hours to keep the traveling public comfortable; in time they organized into one of the most important labor unions in American history — the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters — and their story is far too significant to be a bedtime story. We tip our hat to them, seriously, and let them rest too.

By the eighteen eighties, thousands of Pullman cars rolled across America every night — a scattered, moving city of sleepers, each behind their own curtain. And the sound they slept to is one you can borrow tonight: the old jointed rails were laid in lengths, with a small gap at each joint, and the wheels counted them out. Da-dum, da-dum. Da-dum, da-dum. Mile after mile, the gentlest metronome ever built by accident.

The berth is folded down. The linen is fresh. The porter has drawn your curtain, and the joints in the rail are counting slowly toward morning.

Da-dum. Da-dum. Da-dum.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Fourteen: Cathedrals with Timetables

Tonight we stand still, and look up. This chapter is about railway stations — specifically, about their roofs, which is the sort of specificity this book was born for.

A curious thing happened when the Victorians built their great stations: the age's plainest business met the age's grandest architecture, and nobody thought it strange. A shed for waiting in became a cathedral. The reason was partly advertising, partly pride, and partly a genuine problem: smoke. A steam station needed a roof high enough to hold the smoke of a dozen engines above the heads of the public, and once you must build high, you may as well build magnificent.

At Paddington, Brunel — the broad gauge man; you may sleepily remember him — raised three great vaults of iron and glass, filling his station with pale, filtered light. At York, the roof curves with the tracks themselves, arch after arch after arch, like a tide of iron frozen mid-swell.

And at St Pancras, in London, the engineer William Barlow built, in eighteen sixty-eight, the widest roof in the world: a single arch of iron and glass, two hundred and forty feet across, with no columns at all. Nothing holds up the middle. The two sides simply lean their enormous weight against each other, tied together by the floor beneath the platforms, and have gone on leaning, quietly, without incident, for a century and a half. And beneath that floor, the columns of the basement were spaced precisely to fit the beer barrels of Burton-upon-Trent, because the railway's steadiest customer was a brewery. Glory above; barrels below. That is the Victorian age in one building.

These stations were built for crowds and noise, but they have their own secret hour. Every station is empty at three in the morning. The departure boards blink over vacant platforms. A cleaning machine hums somewhere far off. Pigeons settle in the ironwork, under an arch built to hold the smoke of engines that no longer exist. The station waits, without impatience, for a train that isn't due for hours.

Be the empty station tonight. Vast, unhurried, lit softly, with nothing scheduled and the great roof holding itself up — no effort from you required at all.

The last train left long ago. The boards have gone quiet. The pigeons are asleep in the iron sky.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Fifteen: The Milk Train, and Other Humble Cargo

Passengers think railways are about passengers. Railways have always known better. Tonight we honor freight — the coal, the letters, and above all, the milk.

From the very beginning, the real business was goods. The Stockton and Darlington was built for coal. The Liverpool and Manchester was built for cotton. Passengers were a pleasant surprise, like finding the theatre profitable between deliveries.

Consider what the railways quietly took over. Coal for every hearth in every city, wagon after black wagon, night after night. Fish from the coast, rushing inland in special expresses so that a cod caught off Aberdeen at dawn could be frying in London by supper. Newspapers, printed at midnight, bundled onto trains by the ton so the whole country could read the same morning news — an everyday miracle nobody looked up from the page to notice.

The Post Office went further, and put the sorting itself on rails. In the Travelling Post Office, clerks stood at racks in a swaying carriage, sorting letters by lamplight at sixty miles an hour, all night, every night. The famous trains did not even stop to collect: lineside apparatus swung the mailbags out on arms, and a net on the carriage snatched them in at full speed. The clerks barely glanced up. Sixty miles an hour, midnight, a bag of letters plucked from a pole in the dark — filed under: routine.

But the milk train is ours tonight, because the milk train was the slowest and the earliest. Every evening, from thousands of farms, the tall churns of milk went down to the country stations. In the small hours, the milk train came through, stopping at every halt — clank of churns, murmur of platform voices, and off again into the dark. Slow, cold, punctual, and utterly unglamorous, it delivered the countryside to the city's doorstep before the city woke. For a hundred years, "the milk train" simply meant the train that ran while everyone slept.

Somewhere in the small hours of history, it is always running. The churns are loaded. The van doors roll shut. The lamp swings once, and the milk train pulls away, at its own pace, stopping everywhere, hurrying for no one.

You are not on it. You are the sleeping village it passes through.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Sixteen: The Trans-Siberian, or Distance Itself

Every railway we have visited so far could be crossed in a day. Tonight's railway takes a week, and that is after they made it faster.

The Trans-Siberian Railway runs from Moscow to Vladivostok, on the Pacific coast of Russia: five thousand, seven hundred and seventy-two miles, the longest railway line on Earth. Construction began in eighteen ninety-one, from both ends at once — a method you will recognize by now — and the two ends had to reach toward each other across most of a continent.

The building of it was a quarter-century of patient difficulty. Rivers a mile wide, needing bridges. Permafrost that swallowed embankments. Around Lake Baikal — the deepest lake in the world — the engineers at first gave up entirely, and put the trains on a ferry; in winter, when even the ferry surrendered, rails were laid directly across the frozen lake. Eventually a shore line was blasted through the cliffs, tunnel after tunnel after tunnel, and the lake could be respected from a comfortable distance.

But the construction is not really the point of tonight. The point is the riding of it.

Because the Trans-Siberian is not a journey the way other train rides are journeys. It is a week of continuous rolling. Seven days. Seven nights. The train crosses seven time zones, and refuses to acknowledge any of them: the timetable traditionally kept Moscow time from end to end, so that the clock in the corridor and the sun outside the window drifted apart, hour by hour, until noon said one thing and the sky said another. Passengers stopped consulting both. On the Trans-Siberian, there is only train time, which is no time at all.

And outside the window, Siberia — which is to say, birch trees. Birch trees in the morning. Birch trees at noon. A river, briefly, as a treat. Then birch trees. Travelers describe a particular state that settles in around the third day, when the mind stops waiting for anything to happen and simply rides. The wheels turn. The tea urn at the end of the carriage — the samovar — stays hot around the clock. The provodnitsa, the carriage attendant, sweeps the corridor twice a day exactly. Distance stops being something to cross and becomes something to float in.

That state, third-day Trans-Siberian mind, is where I am taking you now. Nothing is happening. Nothing will happen. The birches go by in the dark, uncounted. The samovar is hot. Vladivostok is days away and none of your concern.

The wheels are turning. The corridor is swept. The clock is wrong, and it does not matter.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Seventeen: Electricity, the Quiet Arrival

Every technology in this book so far announced itself — steam hissed, whistles shrieked, navvies sang. Tonight's arrives silently, which is fitting, because tonight we talk about electric trains, the politest revolution the railways ever had.

The first practical electric railway was small enough to be a garden ornament. In eighteen seventy-nine, at an exhibition in Berlin, Werner von Siemens ran a little electric locomotive around a circular track, towing benches of delighted passengers at four miles an hour. You may notice we have returned to the circular track and the four miles an hour of chapter two. The railways, like this book, begin everything twice.

Electricity's first real conquest was underground, where steam had always been a bad houseguest. London's early tube tunnels, opened with electric trains from eighteen ninety, were possible only because the new motors breathed nothing at all. Then came the suburbs, and the third rail, and the slow spread of wires over the main lines — country by country, decade by decade, at the usual railway pace: gradually, then completely.

What I want you to notice tonight is not the engineering. It is the sound. A steam engine worked audibly — it panted, it labored, you heard every one of its efforts. An electric train simply hums. It draws its strength from somewhere far away — a power station over the horizon, feeding the wire, feeding the motor — and glides off with nothing to prove. The effort still exists; it just happens elsewhere, out of earshot, handled.

There is a lesson for sleepers in that, and I will administer it gently. You do not have to generate tonight's rest yourself, panting and laboring like a steam engine on a gradient. You can be electric about it. Let the effort happen somewhere over the horizon. Stay connected to the wire — your breath, going in and out — and simply draw what you need, quietly, as you go.

Across the world tonight, thousands of electric trains are humming through the dark on exactly this principle. Depots full of them stand cool and silent, charged and unbothered, waiting for morning without a single flame lit.

The wire is live. The hum is steady. The power station over the horizon is doing all the work.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Eighteen: In Praise of the Diesel, Which Just Works

Between glamorous steam and elegant electricity sits tonight's subject: the diesel locomotive. No one has ever written it a love poem. It is the sensible shoes of the railway world. It is also, quietly, everywhere, and this chapter intends to bore you with its virtues.

Rudolf Diesel patented his engine in the eighteen nineties, imagining, correctly, that it would run on almost anything and waste almost nothing. Getting it onto rails took decades of unhurried tinkering — the engine was heavy, and connecting it to the wheels was awkward, until engineers settled on a beautifully indirect solution: the diesel engine doesn't drive the wheels at all. It drives a generator, and the generator feeds electric motors, and the motors turn the wheels. Most diesels are, secretly, electric trains that carry their own power station along with them. A packed lunch, instead of the wire.

The reason diesel conquered, though, was not cleverness. It was the mornings.

A steam locomotive had to be woken hours before it was needed. Someone came in the small hours to light the fire, and the boiler warmed slowly, and the pressure crept up, and only then, after much tending, was the engine ready to consider work. Every evening, the fire was dropped, the ash shoveled out, the boiler washed on schedule. A steam engine was a relationship.

A diesel starts. That is the whole story. Press the button; go to work. It idles patiently for hours, asks for fuel and the occasional filter, and does not need its fire lit or its ashes carried. Accountants, who are immune to romance, saw the numbers and the age of steam ended — not with a race or a trial, but with a memo.

And so the diesel took over the unpoetic everything: the long coal drags, the lonely branch lines, the shunting yards at three in the morning where a single locomotive trundles back and forth, back and forth, assembling tomorrow's trains one wagon at a time, with a soft clank, and a pause, and another soft clank.

That idling rumble — steady, low, unhurried, going nowhere — is tonight's lullaby. Somewhere right now a diesel is idling in a dark yard, utterly reliable, entirely unadmired, ready for a morning that is still hours away.

It just works. So do you. Both of you can idle now.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Nineteen: The Bullet Train, Described Slowly

It may seem against the spirit of this book to discuss the fastest trains on Earth. Do not worry. Speed, described slowly enough, is just another kind of stillness.

By the nineteen fifties, the railway was supposed to be dying. The future belonged to motorways and jets, and respectable opinion said the train was yesterday's machine. Japan disagreed — quietly, and with graph paper.

The old Japanese main line between Tokyo and Osaka was narrow, crowded, and slow. Rather than widen it, Japanese National Railways built an entirely new railway beside it: new standard-gauge tracks, no level crossings, no sharp curves, nothing shared with anything slower. The engineers, several of them former aircraft designers, shaped the trains like aircraft that had sensibly decided to stay on the ground. The line opened on the first of October, nineteen sixty-four, nine days before the Tokyo Olympics. The world called the trains bullet trains. Japan called the line Shinkansen — simply, "new main line," which is the most understated name for a revolution I know.

The trains ran at one hundred and thirty miles an hour, then faster, then faster still, until Tokyo and Osaka were three hours apart and the airlines on that route grew thoughtful. And here is what the speed was actually made of — not daring, but repetition. Every night, when the last train stops, inspection crews walk the line and measure it. Special trains full of instruments ride the rails in the dark, feeling for a millimeter out of place. In sixty years of carrying billions of passengers, the Shinkansen has never had a fatal accident in operation. That record was not set at two hundred miles an hour. It was set at walking pace, at two in the morning, by people with gauges.

Inside, at full speed, there is famously little to feel. The ride is so smooth that the standing test is a tourist ritual: a coin balanced on its edge on the windowsill stays standing, mile after mile. Two hundred miles an hour, and the coin has no opinion about it. The landscape slides past like something being gently unrolled. Mount Fuji appears, holds still politely, and is put away again.

Be the coin tonight. However fast the world is moving — and it is always moving — you can stand perfectly balanced and feel almost none of it.

The last train is in. The measuring cars are out, feeling the rails in the dark. The coin has not fallen.

Goodnight.

## Chapter Twenty: The Slow Trains That Remain

We have reached the last chapter, and I will let you into the secret of this whole book: it was never really about trains. It was about a pace. And so we end with the trains that kept it.

When the great railways modernized, the old machines did not entirely vanish. In nineteen fifty-one, in a valley in Wales, a group of enthusiasts took over the tiny, worn-out Talyllyn Railway and ran it themselves, as volunteers, for no better reason than that they could not bear to see it stop. It was the first railway in the world preserved that way. It has not stopped since.

The idea spread, at branch-line speed, until hundreds of little railways around the world were saved and reopened — not to go anywhere, but to go slowly. On a preserved railway, slowness is the entire product. The train takes forty minutes to do what a car does in ten, and every seat is full, because the ten-minute version has nothing in it and the forty-minute version has everything: the smell of coal smoke, the creak of old carriage wood, a guard with a flag in no hurry at all, tea in a paper cup that tastes better than it should.

The volunteers who run them spend their weekends oiling valve gear, polishing brass, repainting station benches the correct shade of a railway company that ceased to exist before they were born. Ask them why and they find it hard to say. The honest answer might be that some things deserve to be kept at their original speed.

And that is where I will leave you, at the end of our line. Think of everything now rolling through this book behind you, all of it in motion at once: the horse walking the coal cart down from the mine. Puffing Billy at his fifty-year post. The Rocket trundling back and forth past the judges. The navvies' barrows, the signalman's kettle, the golden spike and the iron one. The porter drawing the curtain on a Pullman berth. The milk train clanking at a country halt. The samovar steaming across Siberia. The diesel idling in its dark yard, and the coin standing balanced on the windowsill of the fastest train in the world.

None of it needs you. That was always the most restful fact in this book. The trains ran before you listened, and they will run while you sleep, at every speed, on every gauge, under every signal — all of it checked, all of it watched, all of it running to time.

The flag is furled. The lamp is green. The last slow train is leaving now, and you are already aboard, and there is nothing to do but be carried.

Goodnight. Sleep well. Mind the gap between today and tomorrow.

*The end.*
